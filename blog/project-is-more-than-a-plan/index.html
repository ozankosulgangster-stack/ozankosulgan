A Project Is More Than a Plan

Plans change in projects and in life. The real craft is learning how to test assumptions, ask better questions, and move forward with perspective.

By Ozan Kosulgan July 17, 2026 4 min read

In my professional career, when I first started as a project manager, I tried very hard to create the perfect plan. It had to be consistent. It had to make sense to stakeholders. It had to show that the work was under control.

But in every project, I learned that real life has its own rhythm. Time plans change. Resources change. Budgets change. Priorities change. At first, this was demotivating for me. I kept asking myself: why am I putting so much effort into a plan if it is going to change again and again?

Life taught me that a project is more than a plan. When I say life, I mean it. Plans change in personal life too, whether we like it or not. You make dinner plans, then a friend gets sick and the plan is cancelled. You find the perfect vacation destination, then the prices are too high and you delay the trip. The plan matters, but the plan is not the whole journey.

I started to understand that project management applies to many life situations. You can treat a project as a journey. You can treat it like climbing a mountain. You can see it as a big match. I even treat the World Cup as a project. I joined my son's soccer club pool pick contest, so I make assumptions, validate them as the tournament advances, and accept that there are always surprises.

Planning Still Matters

Project management is a tough job, but it is exciting too. Today, projects are becoming more data-centric, and more AI projects are coming into the picture. This creates new questions. How do you handle model consistency? How do you govern risks that were identified early? How do you define the right boundaries around data, decision-making, and accountability?

It is easy to get overwhelmed. But the same project mindset still helps: break the problem down, test your assumptions, communicate clearly, and learn before scaling.

A Small Garden Lesson

Let me give a specific example from my own life. Recently, I was thinking a lot about weeds in my garden. They keep coming back, and I need to find a solution before my grass is affected negatively.

I started searching different chemicals. One of my neighbors offered a mix that worked in his garden. I liked the idea, but I wanted to validate the result before applying it to my whole backyard.

Then I consulted another neighbor, and he gave me a great way of thinking: "Why don't you test it in a small area and see if it works efficiently before applying it to the whole garden?"

That gave me relief. Suddenly the problem became a phased project. I did not need to solve the whole garden at once. I could run a small test, observe the result, learn, and then decide what to do next.

Ask, Test, Then Scale

I believe communicating and talking with different people always gives us perspective. Project managers are communicators. We ask questions. We challenge ideas. We validate assumptions. We help people see the risks before the whole backyard is treated with the wrong solution.

This weekend, maybe you can think about one problem in your work or life and ask at least two different people for their perspectives. You may hear something simple that gives you relief, or something practical that helps you break the problem into phases.

It could be a good challenge. What do you think?
